Rebuilding Cities with Circular Intelligence

ReBuild Paris transforms urban renovation by identifying, classifying and mapping reusable building components using artificial intelligence.
Based on ETH Zurich Research

How ReBuild Paris Would Work

Inspired by the success of the ETH Zurich pilot project, ReBuild Paris envisions a city-wide initiative to identify and reclaim reusable building components from renovation and demolition sites across Paris. By applying a similar AI-driven approach, we aim to merge architecture, sustainability, and technology in one of Europe's most historically rich urban environments.

The initial phase would focus on the 11th and 20th arrondissements — neighborhoods known for their dynamic urban renewal, architectural diversity, and active construction permits. These zones offer an ideal testbed for large-scale circular economy practices.

Our Process

  • Data Collection: Use Google Street View, municipal archives, and drone scans to collect high-resolution facade imagery.
  • AI Analysis: Apply deep learning models (e.g., Mask R-CNN, MobileNetV2) to detect components like wooden shutters, balconies, wrought iron, clay tiles, and ornate windows.
  • Digital Mapping: Build an interactive map of reusable components per building, ranked by quantity, thermal efficiency, and accessibility.
  • Material Routing: Connect detected components with local reuse warehouses, architectural firms, and new construction projects.

Expected Outcomes

  • Over 4,000 m² of reusable elements identified in two test districts alone
  • A 30% reduction in demolition-related waste for participating sites
  • Creation of a public-facing reuse inventory and digital material passport per building
  • Collaboration between city officials, reuse centers, and architecture students

ReBuild Paris isn’t just a concept — it’s a scalable vision for transforming how cities view demolition, giving new life to old materials and embedding circularity into the urban fabric.

Pilot Project: Zurich

As part of the ETH Zurich project, a facade analysis was conducted across two districts of Zurich (Kreis 4 & 5), comprising over 2,300 buildings. The AI pipeline was applied to extract reusable components such as windows, doors, and tiles using images from Google Street View. Results showed that 76% of detected facade elements were reusable, and up to 4,500 m² of wood cladding could potentially be recovered from a single urban area.
